# Synthetic User Evidence: Vectors Growth Guidance

> **Status: synthetic research input, not observed user research.**
>
> Every participant, behavior, quote, and reaction in this document is fabricated from
> current Kwilt personas, product behavior, and plausible usage patterns. Use it to pressure-test
> frames and generate hypotheses. Do not cite it as validation, customer demand, or evidence that
> a design works.

## Why this evidence pack exists

Vectors are intended to help people notice the kinds of growth their Goals and Activities move:

- **To-do / Activity** — physical doing, execution, embodied or hands-on work.
- **Connection** — relationships, service, support, and collaboration.
- **Mastery** — skill, clarity, craft, learning, and problem-solving.
- **Spirituality** — alignment with God, inner character, integrity, and discipleship.

Today, Goal detail exposes four 0-3 Vector values behind a separate edit state. Chapters can
describe Vectors that appeared in completed Activities. Vector coverage does not currently shape
ordinary Goal or Activity recommendations.

This pack explores three proposed moments:

1. **Goal meaning:** understand or edit what kinds of growth a Goal is intended to move.
2. **Chapter reflection:** notice a pattern or an underrepresented direction without being scored.
3. **Recommendations:** consider attention to a direction with no current Goal or to-do.

It also explores whether Goal editing should require a separate tap to enter edit mode.

Related system frame: [Areas + Vectors Reconciliation](../areas-vectors-reconciliation/00-frame.md).

Nine composite participants react to three low-fidelity concept prompts. The sample intentionally
overrepresents people for whom identity, reflection, drift, and life-system trust are consequential.

### Concept prompts

**A. Goal detail**

Participants see the current four-row, 0-3 display from the supplied screenshot. They first
describe what they believe the values mean. Then they compare the current edit-pencil pattern with
an always-available segmented control and a compact summary that expands when tapped.

**B. Chapter reflection**

Participants read a hypothetical Chapter observation:

> Most of what you completed this month drew on Mastery and getting things done. Connection was
> quieter in the activity you recorded. That may fit this season. If you want a balancing thread,
> you could make room for one small act of reaching out.

They react to the interpretation, its confidence, and whether it belongs in a retrospective Chapter.

**C. Recommendation**

Participants see a hypothetical recommendation triggered by having no active Goal and no open
to-do associated with Connection:

> Connection has less of your planned attention right now. Want to think of one small way to make
> room for it?

They choose among opening it, dismissing it, asking why it appeared, or turning this kind of prompt off.

## Evidence snapshots

### 1. Sarah A — reflection makes the model feel alive

**Observed synthetic behavior:** Sarah notices the `2/3` and `1/3` labels before she notices the
four names. She initially interprets them as progress toward completing the Goal. Once told they
describe the Goal's intended kind of growth, she revises her interpretation but asks what separates
`2` from `3`.

**Synthetic quotes:**

> "I thought two out of three meant I was two-thirds of the way there."

> "The Chapter version is different. It tells me what the month was made of, not whether I passed."

> "I would welcome the Connection question if it came after the observation. I wouldn't want the
> app deciding every part of life needs equal airtime every week."

**Signal:** The four dimensions become meaningful when translated into lived evidence. Numeric
levels without a nearby semantic explanation invite a progress-score interpretation.

### 2. Sarah B — underrepresentation may be stewardship, not neglect

**Observed synthetic behavior:** Sarah's caregiving produces many Connection Activities, while her
creative Mastery Arc is quiet. She rejects a suggestion to add more Connection and is interested in
a gentle Mastery prompt, but only after she can see why Kwilt thinks Mastery is absent.

**Synthetic quotes:**

> "Quiet is the right word. Missing would be wrong. I haven't abandoned writing; I'm caring for my
> dad right now."

> "Ask whether I miss it. Don't tell me the chart needs balancing."

**Signal:** The emotional truth is not “low Vector equals deficiency.” A quiet Vector can represent
an accepted trade-off. A useful prompt distinguishes longing from obligation.

### 3. Sarah C — the data can accuse the user when capture is incomplete

**Observed synthetic behavior:** Sarah has no Connection-heavy Goal, but her unanchored Activities
include meals with friends, church volunteering, and calls with her sister. A Goal-only coverage rule
would label Connection absent even though it is present in her life.

**Synthetic quotes:**

> "If it told me I was ignoring people, I would stop trusting it. I just don't turn relationships
> into Goals."

> "Show me what you counted. Maybe the interesting thing is that Connection happens without a Goal."

**Signal:** Goal coverage and lived engagement answer different questions. The absence of a Goal or
open to-do is not evidence that a growth direction is absent from life.

### 4. Marcus A — direct editing reduces ceremony but raises maintenance anxiety

**Observed synthetic behavior:** Marcus prefers tapping a number directly over tapping the pencil,
changing a value, and saving. But when every value looks continuously editable, he reads the entire
card as another configuration panel he is expected to maintain.

**Synthetic quotes:**

> "The pencil is one tap too many, but the row of buttons makes this look like setup homework."

> "Give me the plain-language readout. Let me tap a row if it's wrong."

**Signal:** Removing the edit-mode tap may improve correction while making the surface feel more
like a form. A read-first, correct-in-place pattern could preserve meaning without advertising upkeep.

### 5. Marcus B — recommendations must compete with real commitments

**Observed synthetic behavior:** Marcus opens Recommended to decide what to do, not to receive a
general life assessment. He dismisses a free-floating Spirituality card, but engages when the prompt
is connected to a Goal he is already reconsidering after reading a Chapter.

**Synthetic quotes:**

> "Recommended means help me choose from what I already said mattered. This feels like the app
> assigning me a new priority."

> "After the review, sure—ask whether the gap is intentional. On Tuesday morning, no."

**Signal:** Vector discovery and daily action ranking are different jobs. A Vector prompt needs an
earned reflective context before it enters an action-oriented recommendation surface.

### 6. Elena A — a comprehensive gap readout can become a shame surface

**Observed synthetic behavior:** With little recent data, Elena sees three quiet Vectors and laughs
uncomfortably. She says the system is describing the months she was sick as a personal-development
problem. She responds better when the Chapter first acknowledges sparse evidence and asks whether
any part of life is calling for attention now.

**Synthetic quotes:**

> "Everything is low because everything was hard. I don't need four reminders of that."

> "You can say you don't have much to go on. Then ask what feels possible now."

**Signal:** Minimum-evidence thresholds and season-aware language are essential. Sparse data should
reduce confidence and intervention, not increase the number of gap prompts.

### 7. Elena B — imbalance can be the right shape of a season

**Observed synthetic behavior:** Elena's current records are heavily Connection and To-do oriented
because she has a newborn. She reads a “balancing Mastery” suggestion as tone-deaf, even though she
still cares about learning. She would save the thought for later if the prompt explicitly permits
“not this season.”

**Synthetic quotes:**

> "My life is supposed to be lopsided right now."

> "Let me say 'this season is intentional' so it doesn't keep trying to fix me."

**Signal:** Dismissal alone is insufficient. Users may want to record a temporary, meaningful reason
for quiet rather than repeatedly reject the same interpretation.

### 8. Nina A — inspectable derivation turns AI interpretation into collaboration

**Observed synthetic behavior:** Nina taps “Why this?” immediately. She accepts a Chapter insight
when it cites representative Activities and distinguishes Goal Intent from Activity Actual. She
rejects the same insight when it is presented as a bare count.

**Synthetic quotes:**

> "Show the intended shape and the lived shape. The mismatch is the useful part."

> "Zero Goals is a database fact. 'You are neglecting Connection' is a psychological claim."

**Signal:** The product can state evidence confidently and interpret meaning humbly. Intent-versus-
Actual mismatch is more useful than a universal balance target.

### 9. Nina B — missing classification must not masquerade as missing growth

**Observed synthetic behavior:** Nina spots that 14 captured Activities have no Vector data. She
expects Kwilt to say coverage is incomplete before recommending a neglected direction. She is open
to approving inferred Activity Vectors in context, but not to silent backfilling.

**Synthetic quotes:**

> "Before you tell me what's missing from my life, tell me what's missing from your data."

> "I would correct two examples. I would not triage a queue of fourteen."

**Signal:** Data completeness is part of the trust contract. Correction should be lightweight and
sample-based, not another inbox.

## Likely realistic tensions for the design loop

### 1. Readout versus scorecard

People are interested in seeing the shape of their growth, but a persistent four-part visualization
can easily become a dashboard. The design needs to communicate pattern and possibility without
implying an ideal total, equal distribution, or performance grade.

### 2. Absence versus insufficient evidence

At least four distinct states can produce an apparent gap:

- no Goal or open Activity intentionally aims at that Vector;
- the Vector is active in ordinary life without being represented by a Goal;
- relevant Activities exist but lack Force Actual data;
- the direction is intentionally quiet in the current season.

Only the first state is directly established by a “no Goal or to-do” rule, and even then it does not
establish that attention is wanted.

### 3. Reflection versus action

A Chapter can make an underrepresented direction emotionally and evidentially legible. A daily
recommendation asks the user to act. Moving directly from absence detection to an action card risks
turning Kwilt from a reflective life system into a tool that assigns a balanced-life checklist.

### 4. Easy correction versus visible maintenance

The current pencil protects a read mode but hides that the model is correctable. Always-visible 0-3
controls eliminate a tap but amplify configuration. A likely middle path is a calm readout where a
row or value can be tapped to correct it in place, with immediate undo rather than a separate editing
mode and save ritual. This is a hypothesis, not a finding.

### 5. Global growth dimensions versus individual seasons

The four Vectors may be durable dimensions, but the appropriate shape is personal and seasonal.
The system can help a user notice lopsidedness; it cannot assume symmetry is the desired outcome.

## Candidate user-voice anchors

These are synthetic composites suitable for framing, not verbatim customer quotes:

> When I look back on a season, help me notice which kinds of growth my real choices engaged—and
> what has been quieter—so I can decide whether that still fits without feeling graded.

> If Kwilt sees that one direction has no place in my current Goals or to-dos, show me the evidence
> and ask whether it matters now; do not turn a database gap into a judgment about my life.

> Let me correct what a Goal is meant to grow at the moment I notice it is wrong, without making
> Vector maintenance another job.

## Design hypotheses to carry into divergence

1. **Chapter-first activation:** A concrete Chapter observation creates enough context for a quiet-
   Vector invitation to feel earned; a free-floating daily recommendation often does not.
2. **Evidence before invitation:** Showing two or three representative Goals or Activities, plus
   data-coverage limits, will increase trust more than a bare distribution or zero-state.
3. **Intent versus Actual is the insight:** Users will find a difference between intended growth and
   lived engagement more useful than a generic “balanced / unbalanced” classification.
4. **Seasonal permission prevents shame:** “That may fit this season” and a durable “intentional for
   now” response will reduce repeated, unwanted gap prompts.
5. **Correct-in-place beats edit mode:** Tapping a readout value to make a small correction, with
   immediate undo, will be understood more readily than a global pencil/save mode without making
   the whole card look like setup.
6. **Plain benefit language beats taxonomy teaching:** Phrases such as “what this Goal grows” or
   “directions in play” may communicate the job better than “Vectors,” even if Vector remains the
   internal model name.

## Counter-hypotheses the loop must preserve

- A direct Recommendation card may work for users who explicitly opt into growth prompts or ask,
  “What am I overlooking?”
- A visible 0-3 control may be acceptable during Goal creation, where the user is already in an
  authoring posture, even if it is too form-like on Goal detail.
- Some users may want a stable overview of all four Vectors across a longer period. The dashboard
  anti-pattern should not prevent a genuinely useful reflection surface, but the job must precede
  the visualization.
- Users may understand the values after one good explanation; the central problem could be timing
  and downstream use rather than terminology.
- Goal and Activity evidence may be too sparse for trustworthy Vector interpretation until Force
  Actual classification is more reliable.

## Recommended frame input

**Primary audience/persona:** `audience-faith-and-values-driven-builders` / Sarah.

Sarah has the clearest positive demand: seeing ordinary action as evidence of who she is becoming
without turning identity into metrics.

**Risk persona:** `audience-life-transition-restarters` / Elena.

Elena exposes the main harm: a growth-opportunity system can reinterpret a constrained season as
failure and create shame at exactly the moment Kwilt should create relief.

**Trust lens:** `audience-ai-native-life-operators` / Nina.

Nina makes evidence quality, missing classification, and the gap between a database fact and a
psychological interpretation explicit.

**Operational lens:** `audience-burned-out-productivity-power-users` / Marcus.

Marcus tests whether the feature sharpens decisions or becomes another taxonomy and maintenance
surface.

**Provisional aspirational design challenge:**

> How might we help Sarah notice the kinds of growth her Goals and ordinary actions are—and are not—
> engaging, then choose whether a quieter direction deserves attention, while preserving seasonal
> permission, evidence-based humility, and freedom from scorekeeping?

## Questions for real research

1. Without explanation, what do users believe each 0-3 Goal value represents?
2. Do users distinguish intended growth on a Goal from growth actually engaged by Activities?
3. Which concrete evidence makes a Chapter Vector interpretation feel credible?
4. When does a quiet-Vector observation feel illuminating, obvious, inaccurate, or shaming?
5. Does “That may fit this season” provide genuine permission or feel like hedging?
6. Would users rather respond “make room,” “not this season,” “already present,” or “this is wrong”?
7. At what moment, if any, should reflection become a recommendation to create or choose an action?
8. Does direct in-place correction feel simpler than a pencil/save mode on a physical device?
9. Is “Vector” understandable and ownable after use, or does it remain internal-system language?
10. What minimum amount and quality of Goal/Activity evidence is necessary before Kwilt should say
    that a direction has been quiet?

## Evidence integrity rules

- Keep the synthetic label attached when excerpting any section.
- Never convert the pattern counts into market percentages or confidence claims.
- Treat every design hypothesis as unvalidated until observed in a real bundled experience.
- In the design-thinking loop, use contradictions and counter-hypotheses—not just the strongest
  supporting quotes—to generate alternatives.
- Before a permanent release, replace or supplement this pack with self-use, TestFlight behavior,
  and direct conversations using the questions above.
