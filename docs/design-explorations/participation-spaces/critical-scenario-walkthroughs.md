# Critical Scenario Walkthroughs

These walkthroughs replay the leading model through its hardest real stories. Each one must explain source and ownership, authority, personal projections, offline truth, exit, and payment without introducing a global Space switcher.

## 1. Maya assigns Charlie a household Activity

**Starting point.** Maya is in My To-dos and creates “Unload the dishwasher.” It begins as Maya's personal Activity. Because The Watanabes exists, the Activity detail includes a secondary **Assigned to** row. Maya chooses Charlie.

**System effect.** Kwilt asks for the minimum context needed to share it. On confirmation, there is still one Activity, now owned by The Watanabes, authored by Maya, and assigned to Charlie. The handoff preview says it will leave Maya's My To-dos and appear in Charlie's. This explicit move avoids pretending a private object can acquire a household assignee without entering a shared boundary.

**What people see.** Charlie sees it in My To-dos. Maya does not. Maya can open The Watanabes or Charlie's responsibility view to see his list, status, and recurrence. Search and Agent may find it for either person within their grant, always labeled with its source. A recurring chore produces distinct occurrences so today's completion does not rewrite tomorrow.

**Offline.** Charlie can complete an already downloaded occurrence offline. Kwilt marks it pending until an idempotent completion receipt is accepted. Maya sees no false completion before sync. Authority changes—reassignment, recurrence changes, or deletion—wait for server acknowledgement.

**Aftermath.** Unassigning an open item returns it to the Space's unassigned view unless Maya explicitly takes it. Removing Charlie closes or reassigns his open work through the exit preview; shared completion history remains. Sponsorship affects Charlie's broader paid experience, not whether the household can resolve his open work safely.

**Result.** This preserves the uncluttered My To-dos promise while letting Maya create from where she already is. The assignee field is contextual: hidden for solo users and subordinate until a Space or shared context exists.

## 2. Grandma shares a recipe, then the family collaborates

**One useful handoff.** Ruth sends Maya a copy of a recipe. Maya gets an independent Person-owned recipe with “From Ruth” provenance. Ruth cannot edit it; Maya keeps it if either person later leaves Kwilt. No Space, membership, or paid seat is required.

**Repeated need emerges.** Later, Ruth wants several relatives to improve a body of family recipes together. At that moment—not during onboarding—she creates **Watanabe Family**, invites contributors, and deliberately moves selected recipes into a live Space-owned collection. The preview explains that the Space will hold the authoritative versions.

**What people see.** Contributors see the live collection from its share or source entry points. Relevant recipes appear in unified Search with “Watanabe Family”; nobody switches their entire app into that Space. Contributor grants permit recipe edits but reveal no other Space capability. A person can fork an allowed recipe into an independent personal copy.

**Offline.** Downloaded recipes are readable with freshness. Conflicting edits sync through recipe version rules; grant changes and ownership moves require the server.

**Aftermath.** If Ruth leaves, Space-owned recipes remain with her authorship in their history. Her private drafts remain hers and stop being visible if she had only live-shared them. If the family wants to preserve stories or memories, the same owner/authorship foundation applies, but that capability must add subject consent, legacy, visibility, and erasure rules before launch.

**Payment.** A bounded contributor can accept, read, and contribute within the collection without buying full Kwilt. A sponsor may fund broader use, but ending sponsorship does not erase the recipes or alter contributor grants.

## 3. Grandma can help with Charlie's Screen Time

**Invitation.** From Charlie's Screen Time access settings, Maya chooses **Add a caregiver**, selects Ruth, and previews the narrow grant: view Charlie's current availability and respond to his exception requests. Ruth accepts using her own identity.

**System effect.** Ruth becomes a scoped participant in The Watanabes for Charlie's Screen Time capability only. “Grandma” may be the relationship label and “Caregiver” the friendly preset; the effective child-and-capability grant is the authority.

**What people see.** Ruth gets a small Charlie Screen Time surface and relevant requests. She cannot see the household's Money, meals, other children, private Activities, or general member directory. Maya sees Ruth under Charlie's Screen Time access and in a centralized Space access audit, not as clutter in every household surface.

**Device truth.** The Watanabes remains the single Kwilt Screen Time policy authority for Charlie's physical device. Ruth acts through that authority; her presence does not create a second rule engine. The device must acknowledge policy or exception receipts before Kwilt says they are enforced. Offline, Ruth can read a dated last-known state but cannot approve a new exception as though it succeeded.

**Aftermath.** Maya or an authorized steward can revoke Ruth immediately. Pending requests close or reroute; a short-lived device receipt prevents stale authority from lasting indefinitely. Ruth gets a calm access-ended notice without private explanation. A block prevents repeated invitations.

**Payment.** A safety-critical, narrow caregiver action sits inside the bounded free envelope. Paying does not widen Ruth's access.

## 4. David invites and later removes a supporter

**Invitation.** On a Person-owned Goal, David chooses **Add support**, selects someone he trusts, and sees exactly what will be shared: chosen check-ins, progress state, and the ability to respond. The supporter accepts. Kwilt does not create a new Space and does not make them a Goal co-owner.

**What people see.** David continues to see the Goal in his personal experience. The supporter gets a bounded shared surface, not David's Plan, Activities, Spaces, or social graph. If the person already exists elsewhere in Kwilt, identity is reused but no prior rights carry over.

**Adult Screen Time variant.** David may instead share an adult Screen Time agreement, current agreement state, and specific exception requests. App names or raw usage require separate explicit disclosure. Kwilt clearly states that adult individual authorization is reversible and may be bypassable; this is chosen accountability, not parental control.

**Offline.** Check-in responses can queue with pending state. Approval, grant, and revocation changes require server confirmation. Requests expire rather than accumulating indefinitely.

**Aftermath.** David ends support from the Goal or agreement. Future access ends, pending requests close, and bounded historical responses may remain as part of David's record. The supporter receives “This support connection has ended,” not David's reason. Blocking prevents re-invitation pressure.

**Payment.** Responding within one support connection can be free. The supporter needs their own entitlement or sponsorship for broader private Kwilt use.

## 5. Jordan leaves one Space

**Starting point.** Jordan participates in their own household, Watanabe Family, and a grandparents' Space. My To-dos and Today already aggregate Jordan's responsibilities across all three with source labels where helpful.

**Exit preview.** In the grandparents' Space, Jordan chooses Leave. Kwilt groups the consequences: personal items and copies they keep, Space-owned contributions that remain, personal live shares that will close, open responsibilities requiring resolution, and pending approvals that will end.

**Resolution.** Jordan completes, reassigns, or returns required open work according to the capability policy. They may fork permitted contributions. The server then revokes participation and emits a receipt.

**What changes.** Grandparents' items disappear from Jordan's projections and search. Jordan's personal data and participation in the other two Spaces are untouched. Space-owned content retains safe authorship. Cached grandparents' data becomes stale and inaccessible according to its sensitivity; it is not silently editable offline.

**Safety variant.** Jordan can choose an immediate safety exit without negotiating work or finding a successor. Their access is revoked first; administrative cleanup enters restricted recovery. They may also block the relationship to prevent re-invites. Billing and stewardship requirements can never force Jordan to remain connected.

**Payment.** If the grandparents' Space sponsored Jordan, that entitlement source enters its stated grace/limited path. Jordan's own plan or another sponsorship continues to cover them without a visible downgrade.

## 6. Blaire takes over payment from Andrew

**Starting point.** Andrew sponsors eligible participants in The Watanabes. This does not make him the sole owner of Space data or the only caregiver.

**Handoff.** Andrew chooses Transfer billing and invites Blaire to billing responsibility. Blaire sees the plan, price, renewal timing, and people covered—no private capability data. She accepts and establishes a valid purchase. The backend confirms the new entitlement source before ending Andrew's.

**What does not change.** The Watanabes remains the same Space. No participation, stewardship, caregiver grant, responsibility, object ownership, or device authority changes. The family does not have to rejoin anything.

**Failure and offline.** Billing transfer cannot complete offline. If Blaire's purchase fails, Andrew's real entitlement remains until expiry. If it has expired, covered people enter the humane grace path and Kwilt tells billing-capable adults what action is needed. Data remains readable/exportable; safety, leaving, and essential caregiver resolution remain possible.

**Safety variant.** Andrew can leave or be removed before billing is repaired. The Space enters grace or billing recovery rather than retaining his relationship as a hidden dependency.

## Cross-scenario conclusion

The six stories use a small set of mechanisms repeatedly:

1. stable Person identity;
2. non-nested named Spaces for durable repeated participation;
3. object-level copy or live sharing for narrower needs;
4. direct or scoped participation plus explicit grants;
5. separate ownership, authorship, and responsibility;
6. unified person-centered projections;
7. server-authorized lifecycle receipts with truthful offline states; and
8. entitlement sources independent of data authority.

No scenario requires a global current-Space switcher, a nested family graph, a universal social layer, or payment-driven permissions.
