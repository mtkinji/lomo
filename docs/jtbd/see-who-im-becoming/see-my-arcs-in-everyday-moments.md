## When this job is hot

The user has named their Arcs and they resonate. But day-to-day life is full of small acts that don't *feel* like becoming anything — paying a bill, washing dishes, sitting in a meeting. Without help, the gap between "who I'm becoming" and "what I actually did today" widens, and the identity claim starts to feel performative.

## What "done" feels like for the user

Small acts get gently re-cast as participation in an Arc when they fit ("that meeting prep — does that belong to Product Craft?"). Maintenance work is named as such, without judgment, so it stops feeling like failure. Over time, more of the user's lived day participates in the story of becoming, and the parts that don't are honestly labeled rather than awkwardly squeezed in.

## Anti-patterns

- Forcing every Activity to be anchored at capture time (violates capture-first).
- Auto-anchoring Activities to Arcs without user confirmation (violates the user's agency over their own narrative).
- Treating maintenance/overhead as a category of failure.

## Notes

This JTBD is the reason **unanchored Activities are first-class** in [docs/life-architecture-model.md](../../life-architecture-model.md) §5. The retroactive-alignment pattern (LOMO nudges to attach unanchored Activities) is the primary realized mechanism.

Risk to watch: the system can over-prompt for alignment and become a chore. The right cadence is "occasional, optional, low-friction" — not a queue of pending decisions.
