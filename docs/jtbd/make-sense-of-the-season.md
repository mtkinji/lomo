## When this job is hot

A week ends, a month closes, a project finishes, a year turns over. The user senses something happened — patterns, drift, breakthrough, slog — but the texture of it slips away unless they actively reflect. Most reflection tools feel like homework or like LinkedIn humblebrag fodder. They want a thoughtful companion observing their life, not a dashboard.

## What "done" feels like for the user

When they read a Chapter, they recognize the season — the rhythms, the drift, the surprises, the patterns they hadn't noticed. The narrative is grounded in what actually happened (counts, time, what was logged), but it's interpreted with humility, not with overconfident psychoanalysis. Drift is information. Strengths emerge. They feel known, not measured.

## Notes

Chapters are **retrospective only** — no future planning lives here. See [docs/life-architecture-model.md §6](../life-architecture-model.md). The mature posture is multiple **Chapter templates** (reflection vs report, weekly vs monthly vs yearly) — each serves a slightly different cut of this JTBD without diluting the core meaning of "Chapter = lookback."

Anti-patterns: KPI dashboards, productivity-clichés ("you crushed it!"), assigning percentages to identity-anchored Goals. Any of these violate this JTBD.
